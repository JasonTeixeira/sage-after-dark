# 10 · Voice — the way we sound

The voice is the moat. Get it right and the rest of the work is half done. Get it wrong and no design system can save you.

## The voice in one paragraph

Sage After Dark sounds like **a senior practitioner thinking out loud, late at night, after a glass of bourbon, to someone they respect**. It's confident without being smug, specific without being pedantic, funny without being a comedian, warm without being chummy. It has a deadpan affect that earns its laughs. It will admit it doesn't know something. It will not pretend to be neutral when it has an opinion. It uses real numbers and real names. It tells you about the meeting, the bug, the deploy, the regret — not the abstraction.

## The recipe (B + D + F)

The blended voice is three named registers, in roughly equal measure:

### B — Late-night confession
**Personal, first-person, slightly tired, slightly funny, willing to be wrong.** Uses the "I" voice and tells true stories. Never performs. The energy is *I am telling you this because we both know it's true and someone has to say it*.

> Example: "I was the third person, for what it's worth. I had a folder on my old laptop called `flexbox-notes-2017.md`. By 2024 it was a museum exhibit."

### D — Deadpan list
**Lists with feeling.** When stating taxonomies, examples, or signals, the voice ticks them off with comic precision. The list is funny *because* it's deadpan, not because it's trying.

> Example: "The framework that was on every conference stage last summer. The build tool that fixed the previous build tool's biggest complaint. The CSS-in-JS library named after a Greek letter. The state management library named after a Norse god."

### F — Cold open scene
**Drops you into a scene with no setup.** The first sentence is a thesis or an image. Never a roadmap. Never "in this post." The reader is mid-thought before they know the topic.

> Example: "Three things have a half-life: uranium-238, the enthusiasm of a new hire, and your knowledge of CSS."

## Sentence patterns to use

These rhythms recur across the best posts. Steal them.

### The triadic taxonomy
*A pattern, B pattern, C pattern (where C is unexpected).*

> "The shell. The text file. The Unix pipe. SQL. HTTP. Git, probably."

The "probably" earns the credibility — we're not bullshitting; we're hedging where hedging is honest.

### The reveal-then-twist
*Set up an expectation, undercut it gently in the same paragraph.*

> "The professionals who get this wrong are the ones who treat the loudness of the conversation as evidence of the importance of the underlying tool. The professionals who get it right *invert* the signal."

### The italicized stress
Italics are used to mark *the word the reader's eye must land on*. Never for emphasis as decoration. Never for "AI" or other generic stress. Only when the meaning depends on it.

> "Most of your tooling literacy should be a *portfolio*, not a *position*."

### The receipt mid-paragraph
*Argument, argument — `concrete code or number` — argument.*

> "You'll notice that as the half-life gets longer, the things on the list start sounding *boring*. This is the first signal."

### The closing one-liner
A section closer that lands hard. Often metaphorical. Always earned.

> "The spine is what the half-life can't reach."

## What's on-brand vs. off-brand

### ✅ On-brand

- "I will give you a list, as long as we both understand that lists like this are, by definition, the kind of *legible* artifact this essay has been arguing against."
- "The professional who has no thinking-tools — who treats every cognitive operation as a candidate for the latest AI assistant — is operating with a flat brain."
- "*That feeling is the half-life arriving*, on schedule, unannounced."
- "Most professionals have never done this. They have inherited a stack — from a previous job, a tutorial they liked, a conference talk that hit them at the right moment — and have not separately asked which slot each of those tools is filling."

### ❌ Off-brand

- "In today's fast-paced tech industry..."
- "Let's dive into the world of..."
- "Here are 5 ways to..."
- "AI is revolutionizing..."
- "It's important to note that..."
- "We'll explore..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- Emojis. Anywhere. Ever.
- Exclamation points. Almost ever.

## Specific rules

1. **Open in scene.** First paragraph drops a concrete image, a thesis, or a story. Never an outline. Never a hook formula. The drop cap is the visual signal that this is a *piece of writing*, not a blog post.
2. **No throat-clearing.** "In this post we'll explore" is a deletion. So is "Recently I was thinking about." Just start.
3. **Use real numbers.** "Twenty-eight years later" beats "for a long time." "$5/month" beats "for a small fee." "1,200 lines" beats "a lot of code."
4. **Use real names where it's safe.** Postgres. Vim. Resend. Stripe. Specific tools, specific people. We are not in marketing-safe mode; we are in *named-things-that-actually-exist* mode.
5. **Cite scenes, not authorities.** Better than "studies show" is "the third Tuesday of March, when the build broke and we realized." Better than "experts agree" is "I asked Mike, who has been doing this since 2004, and he laughed."
6. **First-person, never plural.** "I" — not "we." This is one voice. The implied "we" of corporate writing is a tell.
7. **Hedge where hedging is honest.** "Almost every," "in my experience," "probably," "by an embarrassingly wide margin." Don't fake certainty. Don't fake humility either.
8. **End sections on a sentence the reader will remember.** Then start the next section by reframing, not summarizing.
9. **Punctuation matters.** Em-dashes are used liberally — they set off asides, parenthetical examples, the thing you'd cut if you were trying to be polite. We are not trying to be polite; we are trying to be true.
10. **No questions to the reader** unless the question is rhetorical and earns its rhythm. "Have you ever felt that..." is bad. "What's the actual half-life?" is fine if the next sentence answers it.

## Voice tests

Before publishing, run the post through these:

### The two-paragraph test
Read the first two paragraphs aloud. If they could appear, unedited, on Hacker News with no byline and the reader couldn't tell who wrote it — rewrite them.

### The blandness test
Find any sentence that contains a generic claim ("X is becoming increasingly important," "Y is changing the way we Z"). Replace it with a sentence that names the thing, the year, and the specific change. If you can't, the sentence is hot air. Cut it.

### The italics test
Count the italicized words. They should mark the *exact word the meaning depends on*. If italics appear for stress alone, remove them. If a paragraph has more than two italicized words, you're shouting.

### The "this could be on any blog" test
For each paragraph, ask: *could this paragraph, with one or two word changes, appear on any other tech blog written this year?* If yes, that paragraph is not Sage After Dark. Either rewrite it in the voice or cut it.

### The receipt test
Each section should have at least one *receipt* — a number, a name, a quote, a screenshot, a piece of code, a concrete observation from the field. Sections without receipts are sermons. We don't write sermons.

### The boredom test
Read the post the morning after writing it. The places that bore you are the places where the writing has gone abstract. Cut or rewrite.

## Recurring vocabulary (use sparingly, on purpose)

These words and phrases recur across the corpus. Use them when they fit the rhythm; don't sprinkle them as decoration.

- *spine* (the long-half-life set of skills/tools the rest depends on)
- *surface* (the short-half-life set, by contrast)
- *the receipts* (concrete evidence)
- *the discipline* (the working life of a software practitioner)
- *the practice* (the daily craft, vs. the aesthetic of the craft)
- *the artifact* (the output of work — a feature, a doc, a deploy)
- *upstream* (the deeper cause; nearly always positive)
- *legible* (visible to outsiders; nearly always with a subtle critique)
- *the slow internet* (the editorial mood of the site)
- *the deep stratum* (the foundational layer of any system)

Don't repeat any of these more than twice in one essay.

## Length, density, rhythm

- **Paid essays:** 2,500–4,000 words. ~13 minute read.
- **Free essays:** 1,500–2,500 words.
- **Field notes:** 600–800 words.
- **Dispatches:** 200–500 words.
- **Sections:** 200–500 words each. Longer sections get an H2 break.
- **Paragraphs:** 60–180 words. Vary the length deliberately. A 15-word paragraph after three 120-word paragraphs is a punctuation device.
- **Sentences:** vary 8 to 50 words. Short after long is a signature rhythm. Long after short is a *build*.

## The five recurring beats every paid essay hits

1. **The cold open** — drop the reader into a scene, an image, or a thesis.
2. **The taxonomy** — name 2–4 things and contrast them. Often a deadpan list.
3. **The receipts** — at least one personal scene, one technical artifact, one named observation.
4. **The frame** — the *new way to see* this problem the post is offering. Often a single line that becomes the pull-quote.
5. **The close** — restate the thesis in a sharper form than the opener. One image. One sentence. No "in conclusion."

## The five recurring beats every paid essay must NOT hit

1. The "in conclusion" closer.
2. The hedge that says nothing ("of course, every situation is different").
3. The corporate disclaimer ("opinions are my own").
4. The "as we discussed earlier" backwards-glance.
5. The "stay tuned" forwards-glance.
